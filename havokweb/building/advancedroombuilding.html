Advanced Room Building

  For a complete walk-through, check out the Guide to Building on Havok: these pages are intended as quick-references.

 

V.I: Intro to Advanced Room Building

            Okay, so you’re a master of the exit, and the sector type, and the room description.  At this point, there should be nothing holding you back from creating very nice, tasteful rooms (assuming your English is good and you’ve got a healthy creative streak – if not, don’t sweat it, nothing is impassable).

            But this chapter is about the things that aren’t obvious, that only jump out to the player who really looks into the world he’s playing with – in short, this chapter is about how to impress the type of people worth impressing.  Just because this chapter is advanced doesn’t mean you should skip it.  I fully expect every zone to live up to it’s greatest potential, no matter if it’s your first or fiftieth zone.  But don’t go mad trying to cram everything you find in this chapter into every one of your zones – these things are here to make your zones come to life, not to stifle them.

            Not everything in this chapter is necessarily easy to understand, although I’ve done the best I can to make it easy to follow.  That’s why it’s advanced.  Try your best, though, the tricks in here are what really make a zone shine.

V.2: Extra Descriptions

            Extra descs are a must for any good zone.  There are lots of pretty decent zones out there that have none at all, but all of them could be improved by adding a few interesting extras.

            An extra desc is when you try to look at something in a room – usually something mentioned in the room desc – and the mud has something to tell you about it.  For instance:

            This room is tall, with a steep, conical, wooden-beamed ceiling.  The beams are carved through in staggering detail, and inlaid with silver, which shines brightly against the deep color of the wood.

            Player: exa beams

            Havok: The beams stretch from the floor, up along the walls, and all the way up the ceiling, where they meet at the peak.  They are marked with silver-inlaid carvings.

            Player: exa markings

            Havok: The markings are carved into the rich wood of the beams, and were finished with silver.  Most of the carvings are geometric paterns, or leafy stems entwining with one another, but each beam also has one letter inscribed on it.

            Player: exa carvings

            Havok: The markings are carved into the rich wood of the beams, and were finished with silver.  Most of the carvings are geometric paterns, or leafy stems entwining with one another, but each beam also has one letter inscribed on it.

            Player: exa letter

            Havok: The letters, going clockwise around the room, spell out            S-O-U-T-H in the common tongue.

            Player: exa south

            Havok: There seems to be a secret exit embedded in the south wall.

            Player: open door south

            Havok: You open the door.

 

Okay, maybe I got a bit carried away with that one.  Sorry about that.  But you can see what an interesting bit of flesh extra descs can add to a room.  You’ll note that when the player typed exa markings, he got the same response as he got for exa carvings.  You can set this up with a single extra desc.

 

            To create an extra description, type edit extra (keyword/s).  You’ll be prompted for the text.  Go all out with your extras.  As a player, there are few things more rewarding (other than finding some awesome loot) than taking the time to look around a room and actually finding something there.  Is every player going to take the time to look around?  No.  But some will – and those players will be impressed and pleased with your building, and in the end, that’s what matters.

            To make markings/carvings extra that I had in the example, I would type edit extra mark marks marking markings carving carvings.  By doing that, any of those keywords would bring up the extra desc about the markings.

            You should note that it is not necessary to embed so many extra descs in a single room, nor is it necessary to hide secrets in them.  Extra descs are nice just in their own right.  I would say that you’re doing a very good job if every room has one extra desc, pertaining to the most pertinent/interesting feature of the room.  If every single thing mentioned in your roomdesc has an extra desc for it, you’re a madman (I mean that in a good way), and your zone’s gonna come out absolutely awesome.

V.3: Exit Descriptions

            I mentioned exit descriptions in Chapter IV.5, so I’ll just bring it up briefly here.  They’re very nice.  Don’t forget about them.

V.4: Flowing Rivers

            To make a river that flows – that is, that will carry a player downstream from room to room – use redit to set the sector type to #8, Water NoSwim.  It’ll ask you for speed and direction.  The speed must be a multiple of 15.  15 is fastest, 90 is pretty slow.  You can set it to 0 if you don’t want it to flow.  The direction is – as always – 012345 (neswud)

            Once the sector type is set, you can use edit riv (speed) (dir) to quickly play with the settings.

V.5: Teleport Rooms

            A teleport room is one where the player/s standing in it will occasionally be teleported to another room.  There are a few interesting things you can do with this.  To make a teleport room, use edit tele (time) (to-room) (lookflag).  Time must be a multiple of 10 – 10 is fast, 60 is not fast.  To-room is the vnum of the room the players should be teleported to.  Lookflag should be either 0 (that’s zero), or any other number.  If it’s 0, then the players won’t know they’ve been teleported, unless they look.  If the lookflag is any number other than 0, the players will be forced to look, as if they had just walked into the room.

            Note: You should have a very good reason to use a 0 lookflag.  If something drastic is changing around the players, they will notice, and there’s no reason for them not to.

            There are examples of teleport rooms all over Havok.  The first two that come to mind are at the end of the Curse, and at the end of Cthol.  There are many others, however, and if you’re creative enough, you can come up with lots of ways – not all of them necessarily the way teleports are usually used – to come up with some very interesting features of a zone.

V.6: Tunnel Rooms (AKA “small rooms”)

            Some rooms have limits on how many players and mobs can be in the room at once.  These are called “tunnel rooms”, although they don’t necessarily have to be a tunnel.  To make a room like this, use edit tunn (# of mobs/players).  The command will automatically set the ‘tunnel’ room flag, which is necessary.  Note that the max number you allow the room counts both players and mobs, so if you have, for instance, a room with a moblimit of 3, and you have 3 mobs that load in that room, most players won’t be able to get into it.

            A good example of tunnel rooms is in the Abyss, where you have to run down past those freaky demons, and the merideths always clog up the works.

V.7: Tricky Exits

            There are lots of tricky things you can do with exits, but you should always be careful that the things you do with them enhance the zone, instead of taking away from it.  For example, you can make an exit that leads into a room, but that has no exit leading back.  I can think of any number of good reasons why you might want to do this, but you should always make sure that there IS a good reason.  Remember – you’re trying to make the game fun, not annoying.
