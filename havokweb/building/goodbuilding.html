Good Building: Making Your Rooms Interesting

  For a complete walk-through, check out the Guide to Building on Havok: these pages are intended as quick-references.

 

            A lot of what it takes to do some really good building is to take advantage of those things I mentioned in Chapter V.  But – a good chunk of it comes from something that no online editor can ever emulate, and that’s good ol’ fashioned Human Creativity.  Building is a creative activity.  It requires imagination, dedication, and vision.  Hopefully, that last sentence describes you to a T.  If not, don’t fear, in this chapter, I hope to give to a running start on the path to making it LOOK like you’re imaginitive and creative, which often times is really just as good.

             Let’s start at the beginning.  A good zone is almost ALWAYS the product of a plan.  That is, the builder sat down and mapped out the zone on paper, wrote down ideas for various rooms and mobs, came up with a background story for the zone to explain what’s going on in it, and after it was all sorted out, sat down to build it.  This isn’t always the way it has to be done, but trust me, especially if you’re working on your first zone or second, you will give yourself a huge advantage towards successfully completing a marvelous zone if you take the time to do these.

Tip 1: Map out your zone on paper.

            I can’t stress enough how important this is, especially to newer builders.  After you’ve finished a few zones, you’ll probably have the practice it takes to keep the full idea of a zone in your head over the length of the building project.  A map – even just a sketched out one that doesn’t necessarily account for every room, but rather just highlights the important parts – is essential.  When you get to those moments when you’re just sitting and staring dumbly at the screen (we all have them), you can look down at your map and ask, “Okay, what haven’t I done yet?  Let’s do that.”  A map can not only help you to keep track of the ultimate end of the zone, but it can give you a reference from which to figure out what to do next when you get stumped.

Tip 2: Make notes on your map.

Keep in mind – a good zone will often take a month or two to complete.  If you don’t write down your ideas, you’re in high danger of forgetting them before they make it into the game.  We all want to see what you come up with, so please, write them down so you can remember to put them in.  Writing things on your map such as mob locations, or notes on what a room might contain, can provide you with material to add in to the room desc when you get to building that room.  It will help you to create more unique and descriptive room descs, which is the name of the game.

Tip 3: Visualize your room!

            Many builders run into the age-old problem where it seems like they’re writing the same descriptions over and over and over.  (Special note: DO NOT use the same room desc for multiple rooms.  If you find yourself wanting to do this, ask yourself why you need several rooms to describe this one place which is apparently uniform in appearance.  No places in the real world would ever have the same descs over and over, so don’t do it here.)  Visualizing your room is the best way to create unique and interesting room descs.  Close your eyes and imagine yourself in this room.  Stand in it for a little bit, and take mental notes.  When you get back to the real world (Havok, of course), write down what you saw.  Imaginations are a wonderful thing.

Tip 4: Some ideas for what to write:

            These are NOT supposed to be a checklist by any means.  Instead, use this list as a reference if you get stuck, or if you’re having a hard time visualizing or even imagining the rooms you’re working on.  Use these as a starting point to get the ball rolling, and then go from there.

 

            If your room is inside a building, ask yourself:

            What sort of building is it?  Is it much bigger than this one room?

            Are there any decorations?  Paintings?  Wall hangings?

            Is there any furniture of important note?  (Note: Read Chapter VII.6: Step 5)

            Are there windows?  If so, maybe an extra desc for the window would be  interesting.  (Note: Read Chatper XI, Part 1)

            What’s the construction like?  Is it a mud hut?  Is it a stone castle? Wooden?

           

            If your room is outside, in the city, ask yourself:

            What buildings are nearby?

            What sounds are audible?  Cities are notorious for noise.

            What are the streets like?  Paved?  Cobblestone?  Dirt?

            Are there trees or plants along the street?

            Do the shops have signs hanging out front?  What do the storefronts look like?

 

            If your room is outside in the wilderness, ask yourself:

            Is it a forest?  What sort of trees are there?  Deciduous?  Evergreen?  Are they young trees, or old?  Is the forest thin or thick, can you walk through it easily without a trail?  Is there undergrowth?

            Is is grassland?  Are there any trees about anywhere?  How tall is the grass? Are there any natural bodies, like rivers or streams nearby?

            Is it in the mountains?  Are the mountains jagged and rough, or soft and rolling?  What elevation is the room at?  Is it above the tree line?  Is it all the way up in the glaciers?  In the forested lower parts?  The rocky areas in-between?  What’s the view like?  Can you see any major landmarks off in the distance?

Tip 5: Go crazy.

            Sometimes, if you just focus on one aspect of a room and just let yourself go with it, you can come up with some very unique, very interesting rooms.
